Background

The common cuckoo does not build its own nest: it prefers to lay its eggs in another birds’ nest. It is known that the type of cuckoo bird eggs are different between different locations. In a study from 1940, it was shown that cuckoos return to the same nesting area each year, and that they always pick the same bird species to be a “foster parent” for their eggs.

Over the years, this has lead to the development of geographically determined subspecies of cuckoos. These subspecies have evolved in such a way that their eggs look as similar as possible as those of their foster parents.

The cuckoo data set contains information on a sample of 120 Cuckoo eggs, obtained from randomly selected “foster” nests. For these eggs, researchers have recorded:

  • length: the length of the egg (in mm)
  • breadth: the breadth of the egg (in mm)
  • species: the species of the “foster parent” ((1) hedge sparrow, (2) meadow pipit, (3) pied wagtail, (4) robin, (5) tree pipit, and (6) wren)

Goal: We wish to determine if the type of foster parent (i.e., the species) has an effect on the average length of the cuckoo egg.

Dive Deeper Questions

After completing the lab exercises above, complete the following Dive Deeper questions. Remember, these questions are worth 3 points each and are graded for correctness.

1. Suppose that all of the measurements for the eggs from wren foster parents were misrecorded. The actual lengths of all eggs from wren foster parents should be 1 mm longer. If we reran the ANOVA (with the correct measurements), would the value of the F test statistic increase, decrease, or stay the same. Explain your answer.

Answer: The value of the F test statistic would decrease. Since the wren foster parents egg lengths should have been 1 mm longer for all parents, then the mean would also reflect a mean of 1 mm longer. This would put the mean at roughly 22.1 mm which is much closer to the rest of the mean lengths from other species. This would decrease the variance in the means across the species groups (which is the numerator of the F statistic) and would correspondingly decrease the F statistic.

2. Suppose the original cuckoo data set only contained the observations of three species (the three specific species are not specified here). The plot from Tukey’s follow-up analysis is provided below. Given this plot, what do you think the value of the p-value was for the corresponding ANOVA for these three species? Your options include: greater than 0.10, between 0.10 and 0.05, between 0.05 and 0.01, and less than 0.01. Explain your answer.

Answer: Since all statistics or point estimates taken from the data to create the confidence intervals lie in all three confidence intervals, we already see that there does not seem to be a significant difference in the three species groups for average egg length. Since most of each confidence interval resides in the other two intervals, we would not expect to see a low p value correlating to a difference in means so we can expect the p value to be greater than 0.10.
