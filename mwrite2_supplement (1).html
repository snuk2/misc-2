Background

The Equity in Athletics Disclosure Act (EADA) is a federal law passed in 1994 that was designed to make prospective students and prospective student-athletes aware of a school’s commitment to providing equitable athletic opportunities for its students. Because of the EADA, all colleges that receive Title IV funding are required to make their athletics data publicly available. There are many variables that each college must report – our MWrite data sets contain some of these variables. During this supplement, you will complete two different statistical analyses, outlined below.

Be sure to load in any required packages first!

The first data set, eada, contains a random sample of 169 schools (that compete in Division 1 athletics). For these schools, the following information has been recorded for the 2021-2022 academic year:

4. First, use ggplot() to create a faceted QQ plot for your variable of interest by school type. (2) Conclusion about the population distributions: based on each sample distribution, what is reasonable to conclude about each population distribution? ggplot(data = eada, aes(sample = recruiting_total)) + 
  
  stat_qq() + 
  stat_qq_line() +

       subtitle = "by me",

       y = "Sample Quantiles") +

Observations about the samples: Looking at the sample distributions, we can see that both samples roughly follow the normal line for the theoretical quantiles, thus we can assume that the sample distributions are roughly normal in this data set.

Conclusion about the population distributions: Since the sample distributions are assumed to be roughly normal, we can assume that if the sample distributions are normal, then the population distributions that the samples came from can be assumed to be normally distributed as well.

Conclusion about the sampling distribution: Since both data sets have large sample sizes (78 and 91), we can assert that the Central Limit Theorem has been fulfilled. We also assumed that the population distributions that the data sets come from are normally distributed, so with both these assertions we can assume that the sampling distributions of private and public schools and the recruiting totals for student athletes also are normally distributed, thus we can continue the hypothesis test.
